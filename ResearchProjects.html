Research Projects

I study the physical mechanisms that govern earthquake initiation, propagation, and termination using large-scale laboratory faults to simulate earthquake rupture processes. During my PhD I studied the effect of fault heterogeneities due to variable friction, geometry, and direct fluid injection. During my post doc, I plan to continue this line of experimental work with a focus on induced seismicity.

Induced Seismicity

We do not currently understand the physical mechanisms that drive induced seismicity well enough to mitigate the hazard they pose to society. To address this, I have utilized both laboratory (Cebry and McLaskey, 2021 EPSL; Cebry et al., 2022 JGR) and observational seismology (Cebry et al., under review, JGR) methods. Laboratory work suggests that fluid injection reduces effective normal stress and induces aseismic slip which can then transfer stress far beyond the pressurized region and trigger seismic slip. However, analysis of repeating earthquakes in southern Kansas does not show any evidence of persistent, long-term aseismic slip in the region, despite years of high-rate wastewater injection. These contrasting observations highlight questions of how fluid is transported through faults and/or the bulk rock and triggers seismic activity across scales.

Complex rupture behavior due to heterogeneities

This work considers the effect of fault heterogeneities such as a geometric bump (Cebry et al., 2023 JGR; Cebry & McLaskey, 2024 Seismica), frictional behavior (Cebry, Ke, et al., 2022 Nature Comm.; Song, Cebry, & McLaskey, in prep JGR), or a stepover on rupture mechanical behavior, termination mechanisms, and radiated seismic waves. Results suggest that heterogeneities may play an important role in the frequency content of radiated seismic waves, efficiency of interactions between seismic asperities, and the formation of new faults. Together these studies pose questions regarding which termination mechanisms are most effective at stopping rupture and how we can monitor these on natural faults.
